Haripada Bhattacharyya

Haripada Bhattacharyya (1915 - ?), son of Ambika Bhattacharyya, was born in Popdia, a tiny village of the Boalkhali subdivision of Chittagong of the undivided Bengal. Born in a poor family of a teacher in the elementary Sanskrit school (Tol), he could not afford to go to school and had his basic education at home.

Haripada was the youngest follower of Surya Sen, the hero of the Chittagong Uprising, who did not let him join since he was only fourteen years old at the time.

For Haripada, the opportunity came on 30 August 1930 at the Chittagong football ground, when at the end of a match Khan Bahadur Asanulla Khan, a police inspector was waving the shield from the dais due to the victory of his team. A notorious police officer, Mr. Khan was on the hit list of revolutionaries for a long time. Harihada seized this opportunity; getting closer to the dais he shot at Mr. Khan and killed him; nobody suspected him that a boy of his stature could do this.

Seized by the crowd, he was mercilessly beaten and paraded through the streets, profusely bleeding. His parents were tied to the tree and tortured till they were in their senses; their huts were drenched with kerosene and were put on fire. In the trial, he was spared the death sentence in consideration of his age but was transmigrated for life to the Cellular Jail.

Nikhilranjan Guharoy

A great revolutionary who spent twenty years of his life in a British prison, Nikhilranjan Guharoy(1888-1974) was born in the Faridpur district of undivided Bengal. Nothing is known about the village he was born in, his parentage, early life, education, and his political association.

His name surfaced in the Nadia-Shibpur dacoity case, which is important in the annals of the Swadeshi dacoity for two reasons: the risk involved in the enterprise compared to its yield; and the imprisonment of a large number of revolutionaries. For the revolutionaries, dacoity was the only means to mobilize funds they needed to buy arms and ammunition.

On 30 September 1915, a group of 25 revolutionaries armed with German Mauser pistols attacked Krishnapada Biswas‚Äôs house at the midnight, a rich merchant in Shibpur village of the Kotwali police station in Nadia. On their return journey in the boat, they opened fire at the crowd chasing them which killed three people.

Later, Nikhilranjan along with fifteen others was arrested and transmigrated for life. His co-prisoners in Cellular Jail included Barindrakumar Ghosh, Upendranath Bandyopadhyay, Ullaskar Datta, and Veer Savarkar. He was released along with other convicts during the First World War; only to be sent back to the Cellular Jail again in the Kandi Bomb Case in 1930 till his final release in 1946.

Pramodranjan Chaudhuri

Martyred at the age of twenty-two years, Pramodranjan(1904-1926) was the son of Ishanchandra Chaudhuri, Zamindar of Kelishahar in Chittagong of the undivided Bengal. Nothing is known about his early education. Inspired by Charubikash Datta, he joined the Chittagong unit of the Aunshilan Samiti as a student in 1920; and then shifted to Surya Sen‚Äôs group.

Chittagong revolutionaries had a shelter at 4 Shovabazar Street, Calcutta with its link to the All-Bengal Revolutionary Party hideout at Bachaspatipara Lane, Dakshineshwar in 24-Parganas. In a late-night swoop at these places (10 November 1925), police arrested Pramodranjan, Anantahari Mitra, and others from Dakshineshwar, with a huge cache of a revolver, ammunitions, bomb-making ingredients, and unlawful documents; In the trial, they were sentenced to five years rigorous imprisonment.

In the ensuing trial, Pramodranjan and Anantahari Mitra were sentenced to death; the order was executed on 28 September 1926 in the Alipore Central Jail.

Somnath Bira

Somnath Bira was born in a Kashmiri Pandit family of Rainawari in Srinagar. He was a postgraduate in History from Lahore University and did his research on the topic ‚Äú Kashmir in the Eyes of Foreign Travellers‚Äù. P.N.Jalali, M.L.Misri, Peer Ghiyasuddin, N.N. Rai, and O.N.Trisal, etc some of the famous personalities of the time were his batchmates. Somnath Bira was associated with Sheikh Abdullah and was an active member of the National Conference. The changing political scenario that emerged in Kashmir almost simultaneously with the partition of India finally resulted in the Pakistan-led tribal invasion of 22nd October 1947. When the tribal raiders, abetted and assisted by the regular Pakistan army, entered the border regions of the erstwhile state of Jammu and Kashmir, a wave of terror was swept across from Muzafarabad down to the commercial town of Baramula, at least 25 km from Srinagar. Shops and houses were looted and plundered and set on fire. Women were kidnapped, molested, and abducted. Lurid tales of woe and sufferings of the residents of these areas (mostly Hindus and Sikhs) reached Srinagar in no time which further magnified the all-pervading sense of dread. The havoc created by the severe devastation caused by the raiders in almost all the frontier villages and towns generated a feeling of strange eerieness that engulfed the whole valley.

Owing to the precarious situation prevailing in Srinagar due to the collapse of the administrative machinery, weak state military arrangements, and the Pakistani raiders at the doorstep, the National Conference volunteers were called upon to come forward and render their services in the protection of their motherland. Somnath Bira, an active member of the National Conference with a progressive outlook responded ly to the call and took up the responsibility of preparing some sort of civil resistance as the first line of defence of the valley. Somnath Bira along with some other volunteers of the National Conference played a pioneering role in the formation of Peace Brigade units. Though woefully lacking military training or war tactics, Somnath Bira and his volunteer team acted ly to guard against the Kabali attack.

Referring to the reports of a possible incrusion in Srinagar, Bira mobilized his co-workers and played a lead role in the organisation of a volunteer corps to maintain peace and safeguard the motherland from the impending tribal attack. The response was overwhelming. Within no time, the peace brigade, initially known by the name Bachau Fauj (Protection Force) or Salamati Fauj was organised. Marching and Sloganeering with revolutionary fervor, Somnath Bira and his associates moved down the lanes and streets, bolstering the confidence of the people of Srinagar. Taking inspiration from Bira, a number of young men in the Peace Brigade started offering guard duties on bridges, bazaars, and telephone and telegraph exchanges. They also acted as guides for the army and along with the jawans, they patrolled the streets of Srinagar and sought to defend the main points of entry in the city.

However, the reports of the rapid advance of the mercurial raiders and the continuous migration of the refugees with their woeful stories of the atrocities committed on the non-muslims and their brutal killings created communal tensions in the surrounding region's valley. Around this time, the peace brigade was bifurcated into different units and assigned the task of holding charge of different areas to combat the communal tensions and to stall the march of raiders, in case an invasion occurs. One such peace Brigade in which Somnath Bira was also a leading member, was assigned the charge of the Doda-Bhaderwah area to calm down the communal tensions that had erupted there. However, after successfully accomplishing their duty, when the Brigade members were returning back to Srinagar, Bira was found missing. It is believed that he was shot dead by the hostile forces near a place called Reghi Nallah, somewhere between Doda and Bhaderwah. The exact location where his body fell remained unknown even to his group members and unfortunately, the body of this young and passionate martyr could never be recovered. The life of this young intellectual was silenced while performing his patriotic duties in the service of the nation.

Kedarnath Sahni

Kedar Nath Sahni was born on 24 October 1926 at Thatta village of Rawalpindi. His father, Keshav Das Sahni, was a squadron leader in the army. It was this army background that became the primary source of his disciplined and courageous life.

After completing his primary education, he came to Jammu in 1942 for further studies and joined the Prince of Wales College (now GGM Science College) . It was during his stay at Jammu that he met Sri Premnath Dogra, the chief organizer (Sangha Chalak) of the Rashtriya Swayamsewak Sangha. Influenced by his ideology and working style, he joined the volunteer group, and later, after completing his studies, he made Mirpur the core area of his volunteer activities.

At the time of partition, Sri Kedarnath Sahni was the district pracharak of Mirpur, a district headquarters of the western part of Jammu province which was predominantly a Muslim majority area. Hindus, who were less than ten percent of the population, constituted the business community. It was a center of influential Mahajans who had trading relations with Jammu.

After ravaging Bhimber and Kotli- both the tehsil headquarters within the Mirpur district, the Pakistani raiders reached this strategic town on 25th November 1947. Unfortunately, after the fall of Bhimber and Kotli, Mirpur had been cut off from Jammu. The state garrison stationed there had been running out of ammunition due to constant firing against the raiders. Terror-stricken helpless civilians began to run in panic. However, hardly two thousand people strugglingly managed to reach Jhanger safely while the rest were either killed or taken away as captives. A number of women were abducted and sold in bazaars of Peshawer and Rawalpindi. The repeated request and persuasion with the central authorities for the reinforcement of the Indian army in Mirpur was turned down and was rather diverted to Kashmir region due to the indifferent attitude of the state authorities towards these areas.

In the midst of the barbarity of the Pakistani army, the resonance of a ray of hope during this seething turmoil could be seen in the commendable work of Sri Kedarnath Sahni and that too, at that particular time, when the state troops and the officials lost heart and retreated, leaving behind the helpless civilians at the mercy of the dreaded raiders. It was no other than Kedarnath Sahni and his team of volunteers who saved the lives of thousands of Hindus and Sikhs who were migrating to Jhanger in caravans from Mirpur and other adjoining areas.

During the time of the Pakistani tribal attack, Sri Kedarnath ji was at Sangh headquarters at Deewana Mandir, Jammu. As soon as he got the news of the mass massacre of Hindus and Sikhs in the district of Mirpur, Sahni Ji, with the help of some active volunteers including Sri Gopal Dutt Mehangi, Sri Jagdish Abrol, and others immediately managed the humanitarian aid and food (dal mixed chapattis) prepared in the houses of the local people and packed in small bags for the migrating refugees. Around twenty-five lorries packed with this food, bags proceeded towards Jhangar where the caravans of the people from Mirpur were advancing. The Lorries reached safely to Jhangar where sangha sewaks distributed those dal mixed chapattis to nearly 4,500 refugees from Mirpur and subsequently, helped these caravans to reach Jammu safely where under the supervision of Sri Kedarnath Sahni, the people of Jammu had made further arrangements for providing humanitarian aid to these refugees.

His daunting task of saving the lives of helpless people of Mirpur and adjoining areas is a remarkable example of his sense of duty towards his nation and his feelings of patriotism which is inspirational. Later, he joined active politics and served the nation in various capacities as Governor of Sikkim (2001-2002) and Governor of Goa (2002-2004).

Giani Kartar Singh

Kartar Singh (1902-1974) was born on 22 February 1902 in the Chak No. 40 district of Lyallpur (Faisalabad, now in Pakistan). His family was originally from Nagoke village in the Amritsar district. Following the annexation of Punjab on March 29, 1849, the British government established canal colonies in Lyallpur, and Kartar Singh's family migrated there. He received his early education at the village gurudwara before enrolling in Khalsa School in neighboring Chak No. 41, where he matriculated in 1921. He had a religious bent of mind and during his school days, he led a kirtan jatha, or group of hymn singers, earning him the moniker Giani (learned in religious texts). For further studies, he enrolled at Khalsa College, Amritsar.

The Jallianwala Bagh Massacre profoundly influenced him, and he became interested in politics. When Michael O'Dwyer killed several innocent people in Amritsar, he was there. This event, as well as the martial law conditions in Punjab under which he travelled from Amritsar to his village, had a profound effect on his mind. In 1920, he and a group of fellow students raised funds for Mahatma Gandhi's Tilak Swaraj Fund.

Kartar Singh led a group of students to a Sikh conference in Dharowali village, near Dera Baba Nanak, in early October 1920. Later on, Shiromani Akali Dal was founded as a Sikh representative body on December 14, 1920. He was appointed general secretary of the Shiromani Akali Dal's Lyallpur district unit in 1924. During the Jaito Morcha (February 1924), he was arrested for welcoming and serving langar to a Shaheedi Jatha (martyr's group) on its way to Nabha to participate in the Morcha. He was sentenced to six months in the Cambellpur Jail for this.

In 1926, he was elected to the Shiromani Gurdwara Parbandhak Committee, which had been reconstituted under the Sikh Gurdwaras Act of 1925. In October 1927, he became a member of its executive committee. He led a protest against the Simon Commission's visit to India on October 30, 1928, at the Lahore Railway Station, shouting "Simon, Go Back!" During the Civil Disobedience Movement, he was arrested and sentenced to one year in prison for delivering seditious speeches.

Kartar Singh was elected to the executive committees of both the Shiromani Gurdwara Parbandhak Committee and the Shiromani Akali Dal in 1933. He was elected to the Punjab Legislative Assembly from the Lyallpur district in 1937. In 1943, Kartar Singh proposed his Azad Punjab scheme in response to Pakistan's Muslim League demand. The scheme envisioned a Punjab in which no single religious community held a sway. As a representative of Sikhs, Master Tara Singh, Akali Stalwart, pursued this scheme in political negotiations with the Congress, Muslim League, and British.

Kartar Singh was elected president of the Shiromani Akali Dal in January 1947. On June 3, 1947, Viceroy Mountbatten declared the partition plan. During these turbulent days, Kartar Singh, at great personal risk, assisted in the migration of Sikhs to India, particularly from the districts of Lyallpur and Sheikhupura. On March 17, 1948, the Shiromani Akali Dal working committee, led by him, passed a resolution allowing all Akali members of the East Punjab Assembly to join the Congress. Kartar Singh joined the Congress and was appointed Revenue Minister in the East Punjab government of Chief Minister Gopi Chand Bhargava. He made his mark in rehabilitating the refugees from Pakistan. Here, he was ably assisted by Dr. M. S. Randhawa.

Sardar Gouthu Latchanna

Shri Sardar Gouthu Latchanna who was a veteran freedom fighter was born in Baruva village of the Sompeta Mandal, Srikakulam district in the state of Andhra Pradesh on 16 August 1909. He was a champion of kisans, backward classes, and weaker sections and one of the most prominent leaders of his time. He was arrested at a very young age of 21 when he participated in the Salt Satyagraha at Palasa. Latchanna also participated in the Quit India Movement. He was conferred the title of Sardar for his fearless fight against the British Raj. Having been inspired by Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi's "fast-unto-death" on the issue of untouchability, Latchanna organized "Harijan Seva Sangam" at Baruva. He started a night school in the harijan-cheri, agitated at the district level against untouchability, and took Harijans to a drinking water well at Baruva. He and his colleagues were socially boycotted by the local people. Shri Sardar Gouthu Latchanna was a born leader of the masses, freedom fighter, and social reformer. Till Independence, he fought against the British. After the end of the British Raj, he was on political and social fronts for the sake of farmers, laborers, and the working class.
