Haripada Bhattacharyya

Haripada Bhattacharyya (1915 - ?), son of Ambika Bhattacharyya, was born in Popdia, a tiny village of the Boalkhali subdivision of Chittagong of the undivided Bengal. Haripada was the youngest follower of Surya Sen, the hero of the Chittagong Uprising, who did not let him join since he was only fourteen years old at the time.

For Haripada, the opportunity came on 30 August 1930 at the Chittagong football ground, when at the end of a match Khan Bahadur Asanulla Khan, a police inspector was waving the shield from the dais due to the victory of his team. A notorious police officer, Mr. Khan was on the hit list of revolutionaries for a long time. His parents were tied to the tree and tortured till they were in their senses; their huts were drenched with kerosene and were put on fire. In the trial, he was spared the death sentence in consideration of his age but was transmigrated for life to the Cellular Jail.

However, after successfully accomplishing their duty, when the Brigade members were returning back to Srinagar, Bira was found missing. It is believed that he was shot dead by the hostile forces near a place called Reghi Nallah, somewhere between Doda and Bhaderwah. The exact location where his body fell remained unknown even to his group members and unfortunately, the body of this young and passionate martyr could never be recovered. The life of this young intellectual was silenced while performing his patriotic duties in the service of the nation.

Kedarnath Sahni

Kedar Nath Sahni was born on 24 October 1926 at Thatta village of Rawalpindi. His father, Keshav Das Sahni, was a squadron leader in the army. It was this army background that became the primary source of his disciplined and courageous life.

After completing his primary education, he came to Jammu in 1942 for further studies and joined the Prince of Wales College (now GGM Science College) . It was during his stay at Jammu that he met Sri Premnath Dogra, the chief organizer (Sangha Chalak) of the Rashtriya Swayamsewak Sangha. At the time of partition, Sri Kedarnath Sahni was the district pracharak of Mirpur, a district headquarters of the western part of Jammu province which was predominantly a Muslim majority area. Hindus, who were less than ten percent of the population, constituted the business community. It was a center of influential Mahajans who had trading relations with Jammu.

After ravaging Bhimber and Kotli- both the tehsil headquarters within the Mirpur district, the Pakistani raiders reached this strategic town on 25th November 1947. Unfortunately, after the fall of Bhimber and Kotli, Mirpur had been cut off from Jammu. The state garrison stationed there had been running out of ammunition due to constant firing against the raiders. Terror-stricken helpless civilians began to run in panic. However, hardly two thousand people strugglingly managed to reach Jhanger safely while the rest were either killed or taken away as captives. A number of women were abducted and sold in bazaars of Peshawer and Rawalpindi. The repeated request and persuasion with the central authorities for the reinforcement of the Indian army in Mirpur was turned down and was rather diverted to Kashmir region due to the indifferent attitude of the state authorities towards these areas.

In the midst of the barbarity of the Pakistani army, the resonance of a ray of hope during this seething turmoil could be seen in the commendable work of Sri Kedarnath Sahni and that too, at that particular time, when the state troops and the officials lost heart and retreated, leaving behind the helpless civilians at the mercy of the dreaded raiders. It was no other than Kedarnath Sahni and his team of volunteers who saved the lives of thousands of Hindus and Sikhs who were migrating to Jhanger in caravans from Mirpur and other adjoining areas.

During the time of the Pakistani tribal attack, Sri Kedarnath ji was at Sangh headquarters at Deewana Mandir, Jammu. As soon as he got the news of the mass massacre of Hindus and Sikhs in the district of Mirpur, Sahni Ji, with the help of some active volunteers including Sri Gopal Dutt Mehangi, Sri Jagdish Abrol, and others immediately managed the humanitarian aid and food (dal mixed chapattis) prepared in the houses of the local people and packed in small bags for the migrating refugees. Around twenty-five lorries packed with this food, bags proceeded towards Jhangar where the caravans of the people from Mirpur were advancing. The Lorries reached safely to Jhangar where sangha sewaks distributed those dal mixed chapattis to nearly 4,500 refugees from Mirpur and subsequently, helped these caravans to reach Jammu safely where under the supervision of Sri Kedarnath Sahni, the people of Jammu had made further arrangements for providing humanitarian aid to these refugees.

His daunting task of saving the lives of helpless people of Mirpur and adjoining areas is a remarkable example of his sense of duty towards his nation and his feelings of patriotism which is inspirational. Later, he joined active politics and served the nation in various capacities as Governor of Sikkim (2001-2002) and Governor of Goa (2002-2004).

Giani Kartar Singh

Bhagat Singh

Bhagat Singh was born in the Rurhiwal village of Amritsar district. His father‚Äôs name was Bur Chand. As a soldier, he joined the 23rd Cavalry (Frontier Force), the personal escort of Punjab Governor Michael O'Dwyer. In the last days of 1914, he met Prem Singh of Sur Singh village, a Ghadar Party member, and agreed to join the fight for Indian independence. The Ghadar Party was founded on America's Pacific Coast with the stated goal of freeing India from the yoke of British slavery. The idea for the Ghadar Party came from Indian immigrants who had moved to the Pacific Coast of North America to make a living. The Ghadar Movement arose in response to colonial authorities' racist-cultural attitudes, discriminatory immigration policies, and distorted legal procedures used to restrict Indian citizens' entry into America and Canada. It was a one-of-a-kind effort by worthy sons of the Indian motherland. One of the Ghadar Party's main goals was to persuade Indian troops to join the movement.

The British were mass-recruiting young Indians to send to the front lines in World War I. The Ghadar Party intended to use Indian soldiers to overthrow British rule. The sowars of the 23rd Cavalry were persuaded to march out and join the armed rebellion at the appointed hour. Bhagat Singh and his colleagues sent a sword to the movement's leaders as a symbol of their assurance. The Ghadar Party dispatched emissaries to every cantonment in Northern India. Prem Singh of Sur Singh village met Bhagat Singh and confided in him. The general uprising was scheduled for November 30, 1914. The date was later rescheduled. At the time, British officers were completely unaware of the 23rd Cavalry Men's involvement in the Ghadar Party's planned uprising. Sucha Singh of Chola Sahib, however, and two other sowars were punished for their unscheduled absence from the cantonment. The British also put an end to the 21 February 1915 uprising plan.

Meanwhile, the 23rd unit was assigned to the United Provinces (UP). On May 13, 1915, the Sikh soldiers of the 23rd Cavalry were being shipped to the war front from Nowgong Cantonment in United Province (now MP). On the way, bombs exploded in a wooden box belonging to one of the soldiers at Harpalpur Station (MP). This explosion revealed to the officials the sowars' plan to join the Ghadar revolution. Others were later arrested, and their ties to the Ghadar Party were discovered. As a result, eighteen men were detained. In Dagshai, near Shimla, a court martial was held. Bhagat Singh received the death penalty. On September 3, 1915, he was hanged in Ambala Jail. In his last time, the British were even forbidden from meeting him with his family members.
